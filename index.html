About the Workshop

Spatial data is the substrate of modern scientific discovery, from climate projections and Earth-observation foundation models to autonomous-vehicle perception, multiplexed tissue imaging, and material microscopy. Yet this substrate is distorted. Ships go dark. GPS is spoofed. Earth observation suffers projection error, sensor noise, and labelled-data scarcity. Multiplexed Immunofluorescence (MxIF) and spatial-transcriptomics pipelines accumulate registration drift and batch effects. Material-microscopy images are corrupted by beam damage and simulation-to-experiment domain gaps.

This workshop unifies two pillars that together make spatial AI trustworthy enough to accelerate science. The first pillar is distortion-aware spatial AI: a taxonomy of denial-based, additive, and deception-based distortion, paired with physics-informed priors, world-model reasoning, and human-in-the-loop verification. The second pillar is compute-grounded spatial agents: agents that ground reasoning in deterministic spatial computation (scene graphs, entropy-guided reflection, self-healing pipelines) and automate scientific discovery loops across Earth, biomedical, and material sciences.

Outputs include a community white paper, the DASA + Agents Bench proposal, and an open directory of scientific problems requiring distortion-aware spatial agents.

Two Pillars

Pillar A

Distortion-Aware Spatial AI

Detect, reason about, and recover from three forms of distortion in scientific spatial data:

  • Denial-based (subtraction): AIS dark fleets, GPS jamming, MxIF channel dropouts, missing satellite passes.
  • Additive noise: sensor noise, projection error, registration drift, autofluorescence, beam damage.
  • Deception-based (substitution): GPS spoofing, trajectory deepfakes, synthetic-image attacks, simulation-to-experiment gaps.

Methods: space-time prisms, kinematic priors, physics-informed diffusion, geographically-weighted regularizers, world-model reconstruction error, human-in-the-loop filter-and-refine.

Pillar B

Compute-Grounded Spatial Agents

Agents that LLMs alone cannot deliver, because language models hallucinate distances, miscount objects, and lose track of spatial relations. Compute-grounding solves this by separating deterministic spatial computation from generative reasoning:

  • Compute-Grounded Reasoning (CGR): extract entities and positions, compute spatial facts deterministically, let LLMs reason only over verified facts.
  • Structured spatial scene graphs: queryable graphs with typed relations and computed distances.
  • Entropy-guided reflection: confidence routing across tiered models.
  • Self-healing ML pipelines: parse error, fix code, retry; never zero-score.

Targets: lab-automation agents, multi-modal spatial QA, scientific ML automation (MLE-Bench style), robotic-microscope path planning, autonomous-vehicle perception agents.

Bridge: distortion-aware methods are the substrate that makes spatial agents trustworthy enough for scientific deployment. The two pillars are co-equal and mutually reinforcing.

Topics of Interest

  1. T1. Distortion taxonomy across scientific spatial data. Denial, additive, deception, anchored on the dissertation Distortion-Aware Spatial Data Science.
  2. T2. Physics-informed generative and trajectory models. Pi-DPM, GCDM, Kriging-informed Conditional Diffusion, kinematic priors, variogram regularizers, conservation laws.
  3. T3. Physics-Guided Foundation Models for the Earth system. GraphCast, FourCastNet, Pangu, Prithvi, DOFA, SatMAE++, CROMA, Scale-MAE, CorrDiff, StormCast, plus trajectory FMs (UniTraj, UrbanDiT, TrajFM, GeoDiffusion).
  4. T4. Compute-grounded spatial agents. Compute-Grounded Reasoning, scene-graph engines, entropy-guided reasoning, self-healing ML pipelines, cross-provider strong-tier routing.
  5. T5. Spatial agents for scientific discovery automation. Lab-automation agents, multi-modal spatial QA, MLE-Bench-style research agents, autonomous-driving and ocean-science agents.
  6. T6. Domain frontiers.
    • Biomedical: Multiplexed Immunofluorescence (MxIF) and adjacent platforms (CODEX, MIBI, Vectra Polaris, PhenoCycler-Fusion, Visium HD, Slide-seq, Stereo-seq, MERFISH); cell segmentation under registration drift, neighborhood-graph construction, brain-atlas spatial transcriptomics (Allen Brain Atlas, BICCN).
    • Material science: crystal structure prediction (Materials Project, OQMD, MatterGen, GNoME), MOF discovery (the Yaghi line; AIAS+ host alignment), STEM/TEM microscopy denoising, grain-boundary and defect mapping, beam-damage compensation, simulation-to-experiment domain-gap closure via PGFMs constrained by DFT energies.
    • Earth and autonomy: autonomous-vehicle perception, sanctions enforcement and dark-fleet detection, climate downscaling, ocean science.

Submission

Two tracks:

  • Long papers (8 pages + unlimited references). Mature methodological or empirical contributions. ~6 accepted; oral slots in contributed sessions.
  • Short papers / extended abstracts (4 pages + references, or 2-page extended abstracts). Early-stage ideas, position papers, application showcases, benchmark proposals. ~25–30 accepted; poster slots.

Anonymous double-blind review. Three reviewers per paper, scoring on three axes (novelty, technical quality, relevance to the two pillars). Submissions via OpenReview (portal opens July 1, 2026).

Non-archival relative to AIAS+ proceedings; authors retain rights to extend to NeurIPS, ICML, SIGSPATIAL, KDD, ICLR, or Nature Methods.

Travel scholarships available for up to 10 students from underrepresented institutions or Global-South affiliations, contingent on a modest NSF Conference grant currently in preparation.

Dual-Use, Ethics, and DEI

Dual-use. Trajectory analysis and Earth observation have dual-use applications, supporting humanitarian missions (illegal-fishing detection, sanctions enforcement, search and rescue) and posing surveillance risks. Submissions whose primary deployment is offensive military targeting will not be accepted. Defensive applications are explicitly welcomed: sanctions enforcement, marine-reserve protection, autonomous-vehicle safety, biomedical spatial omics, materials discovery.

Diversity, equity, and inclusion. Travel scholarships for up to 10 students from underrepresented institutions or Global-South affiliations. Active solicitation of early-career and Global-South reviewers (30 percent quota on the program committee). Code of conduct adopted from the ACM and NeurIPS standards.

Sustainability. Electronic-only registration packets; published carbon-per-meal estimates; opt-in attendee carbon offset; hybrid streaming to reduce cross-country travel; recordings of all sessions for post-workshop access.
